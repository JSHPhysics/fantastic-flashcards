1. Overlays

Things that sit on top of the page and dim or hide what's underneath.

Modal  / Dialog

A box that appears in front of the rest of the page, with a dimmed layer behind it that you have to dismiss before you can do anything else. It's "modal" because it blocks the rest of the app until you close it. The "Are you sure you want to delete this deck?" pop-up is one. The theme shop is one too.

Live example

Latin Verbs (42 cards) and everything inside it will be removed.

Words that mean the same thing here: "dialog", "popup", "pop-up", "modal box". In code it's called Dialog. Claude usually says "dialog".

Backdrop  / Scrim

The dim, slightly-blurred layer that sits between a modal and the rest of the page. Tapping it usually closes the modal. It's not a thing on its own — it always comes with a modal.

Live example (just the backdrop)

Words that mean the same thing: "scrim", "overlay" (loosely), "veil". Claude usually says "backdrop".

Sheet  / Bottom sheet

The same idea as a modal, but it slides up from the bottom of the screen and docks there instead of floating in the middle. In this app, every modal turns into a sheet automatically when the screen is narrow (i.e. on a phone). On iPad and desktop it floats in the centre instead.

Don't confuse with: a "drawer" — drawers slide from the side, not the bottom. The app doesn't have drawers.

Popover

A small floating panel attached to a specific element on the page — like a little balloon that points to the thing it's about. Different from a modal because it doesn't dim the page, doesn't block you, and is tied to a specific spot.

Drawer  / Side panel

A panel that slides in from the left or right edge of the screen. Common in mail apps and admin tools.

Tooltip

A tiny floating label that shows up when you hover over (or long-press) something. Usually just a few words.

2. Buttons

The app has four button styles. The style decides which job a button is for.

Primary button

Solid navy with cream text. Used for the one main action on a screen — "Save", "Start studying", "Add deck". There should normally be only one primary button visible at a time.

Live example

Secondary button

Bordered, navy text on a pale background. Used for "second choice" actions next to a primary — "Cancel", "Keep", "Edit". Quieter than primary but still clearly tappable.

Live example

Ghost button

No border, no fill — just text that's tappable. Used when a button needs to be present but shouldn't draw attention. The row-level action buttons inside lists are usually ghost.

Live example

Danger button

Red fill, cream text. Used only for actions that destroy something — "Delete deck", "Reset progress". The colour is there as a warning, so it should never be the easy default.

Live example

Button sizes

Two sizes: medium (default, used almost everywhere — at least 44pt tall so a finger can hit it) and small (only for very tight rows). If Claude says "small button", expect the shorter, less-tall one.

Live example

FAB  (Floating Action Button)

The circular navy button anchored to the bottom-right of the screen, just above the tab bar. There's only ever one on screen, and it does the single most important "create" action for that screen (e.g. add a new deck on the Home screen).

Live example

Rating buttons

The four colour-coded buttons that appear under a card during study: Again (red), Hard (orange), Good (green), Easy (blue). Tapping one tells the spaced-repetition algorithm how well you remembered the card.

Live example

4. Pills, chips & badges

All three words mean roughly the same shape. The differences are mostly which word feels right in context.

Chip

A small, rounded label — pill-shaped — that shows a single short piece of info. In this app: the "24 MB" media-size chip on a deck, the "🔥 12 days" streak chip, the "5 due" count. Chips are read-only labels; they're not buttons (though they can sometimes be tappable to filter).

Live example
24 MB 5 due 🔥 12 days Custom Study

CLAUDE.md note: "chip" is in the list of jargon to avoid in user-facing text. We use it freely in our chats, but the app's own copy says "label" or just shows the content directly.

Badge

Same shape as a chip, but Claude tends to say "badge" when it's inline next to text — e.g. a "Custom Study" badge stuck next to a session title in the top bar. Some designers reserve "badge" for very tiny number bubbles (like an unread count). Either way: same shape, slightly different vibe.

Pill

"Pill" is more of a shape word than a component word — it describes anything with fully-rounded ends. A chip is pill-shaped. A toggle can be pill-shaped. If Claude says "make it a pill", they mean the shape.

Tag

Different. A tag is a keyword you attach to a card so you can filter or search by it — like "irregular", "year-9", "exam-1". The visual shape it renders as is a chip, but the word "tag" refers to the content, not the shape.

5. Notifications

Short messages that appear, then go away (or are dismissed). Lots of overlap in the words used here.

Banner

A horizontal strip pinned to the top or bottom edge of the screen with a short message and usually a "dismiss" X. It stays until you dismiss it or the condition that triggered it goes away. Examples: the "A new version is ready, reload?" strip, the "Back up your decks" nudge.

Live example
A new version is ready — tap to reload.

Toast  / Snackbar

Same idea as a banner, but it auto-dismisses after a few seconds and usually appears near the bottom of the screen. "Snackbar" is just Google's word for the same thing.

Live example
Saved.

Heads up: the app doesn't have toasts yet — only banners. If Claude says "we'll show a toast", that'd be a new pattern.

Nudge

A specifically-soft banner — never an alert, never blocking — that prompts you to do something helpful (like back up your decks). Same visual shape as any banner, just a friendlier tone. "BackupNudge" is one in the code today.

8. Gestures & actions

What the user physically does. The app is iPad-first, so Claude says "tap" by default and treats mouse "click" as the same thing.

Tap

A quick finger press and release. On desktop, "click" with the mouse means the same thing. Claude says "tap" by default.

Click

Mouse-equivalent of tap. Same outcome. Sometimes Claude says "click" on desktop-specific notes.

Long-press  / Hold

Press and hold without moving for about half a second. Often used to open extra options. Equivalent to right-click on desktop.

Swipe

A quick slide of the finger across the screen. The app doesn't lean on swipes; if Claude mentions one, it'd be a deliberate new gesture.

Drag

Press, hold, and move. Used for reordering, like dragging a deck up the list.

Scroll

Sliding the contents of a panel up/down. Not the same as swipe (which is a quick flick that triggers an action).

Pinch

Two fingers spreading apart or coming together to zoom. The app doesn't use pinch directly — it's a browser-level gesture.

Hover

Mouse moving over a thing without clicking. Touch devices don't really have hover — buttons usually skip a "hover" state on iPad and just react when tapped.

9. App concepts

Words about flashcards and how the app works — not about what's on screen.

Card  (flashcard sense)

One question-and-answer pair. The thing you actually study. Lives inside a deck. Different from the UI "card" — when context is ambiguous, Claude will say "flashcard" or "deck card" vs "UI card".

Live example
Latin → English
amō
I love

Deck

A group of cards you study together. Decks can contain other decks (those nested ones are sub-decks).

Sub-deck

A deck nested inside another deck. E.g. "Verbs" inside "Latin". When you study a parent deck, you study all its sub-decks too.

Due card

A card the schedule says you should review today (or sooner). "Due today" is the count shown in the green chip next to a deck. When you tap Study, you go through the due cards.

Maturity

How well-learned a card is. A mature card is one the algorithm reckons you'll remember for more than 21 days. Learning cards are new or recently forgotten and come back often. New cards have never been reviewed.

FSRS

The spaced-repetition algorithm the app uses. Stands for "Free Spaced Repetition Scheduler". It decides when each card next becomes due based on how you've rated past reviews. You don't need to know the maths — just that it picks the next date.

FSRS rating  (Again / Hard / Good / Easy)

Your answer after you reveal a card. Tells the algorithm how it went so it can pick the right next date.

  • Again — didn't remember. Card comes back soon.
  • Hard — got it but it was a struggle.
  • Good — normal recall.
  • Easy — instant. Card waits longer next time.

Session

One sitting of study: you tap Study, you go through some cards, you finish (or quit). One session = one of those. (Not to be confused with "Session 4" or "Session 10" in Claude's old code comments — those refer to development phases and should never appear in user-facing copy.)

Custom Study

An alternative to the normal "study what's due" session. You pick decks, tags, card types, max cards, etc. Used for exam-cram or focused practice.

Streak

The number of consecutive days you've reviewed at least one card on. One missed day usually resets it, except you get one "rest day" per streak as a buffer.

Leech

A card you keep getting wrong no matter how many times it comes round. The algorithm flags it so you can decide whether to suspend it, rewrite it, or move it to a more focused deck.

Coins

The app's in-app currency, earned through study and daily actions (like the 5 coins for the first backup of the day). Spent in the shop on themes and fonts.

Build ID

The short identifier (e.g. ab465d5) shown under Settings → About. It's how you confirm that the build you just pushed is the one running on your phone. Claude includes it in chat replies when a build has landed on the live site.

Backup

A file containing all your decks, cards, history, and media — saved by you, by hand, to a place you choose (OneDrive, Files, anywhere). The app has no server, so backups are how you move between devices and survive a wipe.

10. Design-system words

Words Claude uses to describe how things look. Useful for pushing back when a suggestion doesn't feel right.

Surface

The colour of a panel's background. In light mode it's white; in dark mode it's a deep blue-grey. Whenever Claude says "on the surface", they mean against this background.

Surface colour

Ink

Foreground colours — text, borders, icons. Comes in five shades from ink-100 (very pale, for backgrounds) to ink-900 (almost black, for primary text). Picking ink-700 vs ink-500 is how Claude makes text feel more or less prominent.

100 → 900

Navy, Cream, Gold

The three brand colours. Navy is the primary button colour and headings; cream is the app's background; gold is the accent (rank-up shimmer, streak chip, etc.).

Navy
Cream
Gold

Padding  vs Margin

Padding = space inside a box, between its edge and the content. Margin = space outside a box, between it and its neighbour. "More padding" makes the box look roomier; "more margin" pushes the box away from things around it.

Live example
Small padding
Lots of padding

Gutter

The gap between items in a row or grid. "Tighter gutters" = things closer together; "wider gutters" = more space between them.

Stack

A vertical column of items with consistent spacing between them. When Claude says "stack the buttons" they mean lay them out top-to-bottom instead of side-by-side.

Tap target

The minimum size something needs to be for a finger to hit it comfortably. The app uses 44pt (about 2.75rem) as the minimum — anything smaller and you'd start missing taps.

Theme  / Palette

A complete set of colours that gets applied to the whole app at once (Light, Dark, Warm, Forest, Ocean…). Switching a theme repaints every surface and ink shade.

Token

A named value Claude uses in code instead of a raw number — "rounded-xl" instead of "20 pixels of corner radius", or "ink-700" instead of a specific grey. Tokens let a theme change everything at once.
